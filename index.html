About

Hi, I'm Chuck. I'm a linguist and researcher passionate about how, as human beings, our embodied experiences shape our expectations about the world and how to use these expectations to inform design: from creating a new communication system from scratch and swiping behavior on an app to carefully wording advertizing copy to clearly communicate business objectives.

I'm a research generalist, with experience in everything from neuroimaging, information architecture, naming research, to in-depth literature reviews.

Would you like to know more?

I'm a linguistic researcher at Lexicon Branding Inc., a boutique brand name consulting firm in sunny Sausalito, California. My job involves thinking deeply about how brand names will sound in different language markets (e.g., Will "AmerisourceBergen" be pronounced equally well in Arabic, Japanese and Turkish? Answer: no way ), assessing the international community's cultural and embodied relationships to names (e.g., Why would "4th Generation" be a fatal brand name in China? Answer: "Four" and "Die" sound similar in Mandarin), and figuring out how/why people have positive (or negative) associations with completely made-up names (e.g., Why does "Moniker" give French speakers pause? Answer: It sounds like the word niquer ("to fuck")). To these ends, I apply both experimental and computational methods.

I also head up Lexicon's consumer naming research, where I develop, deploy, and analyze consumer surveys to assess the "Day 0" brand equity a name inately carries. I have also helped clients quantitatively reorganize their brand architectures, pick the right product descriptor (Is 'Pro' more premium than 'Plus'?), and align creative copy with company values through qualitative interviews.

I earned my PhD in Linguistics from Purdue University, where I studied with Ronnie Wilbur in the Sign Language and Linguistics Lab. My PhD work focused on the emergence of grammar in language via iconicity, or, a motivated connection between how a word (or sign or gesture) looks or sounds like and what it means. My dissertations explores visual features that guide the interpretation of signs and gestures in novel communication settings, like when a Deaf signer and a hearing non-signer must communicate. Think: Would you cup your hands to order another beer at the bar? (Answer: No way and no sign language does this either). By applying modified linguistic tests to silent gesture, my goal is to characterize how structure arises in developing sign languages and make inferences about how language emerged in humans.

Prior to that, I focused on linguistic constraints on how movement is used in ASL, making critical comparisons between information available in depicting constructions in American Sign Language and information available in drawings, maps, and gesturing. Think: Can you guide someone to the nearest bus station without using your hands?

In my free time, I'm still picking at outstanding puzzles surrounding how language emerges in the visual modality (early sign languages). See Publications below to see what I've been up to. Riding my bike and tending to my small but mighty garden make me happy. In rainier months, I burn through classic and world cinema with my cat, Win-Win.

Theses

Bradley, C. (2019). Transparency of transitivity in pantomime, sign language [Full ↗] [ Summary]

The goal of the project was to uncover similarities in form-meaning correspondence between sign language and pantomime with respect to transitivity coding. In brief, I elicited pantomimes and classifier constructions (a subset of highly imagistic signs in American Sign Language [ASL]) from non-signers and a signer, respectively, that show the manipulation or movement of everyday objects. I annotated these productions for phonetic (purely visual) and phonological (visual, but organized by the grammar of ASL) features and correlated them with transitivity labels (i.e., manipulation: transitive; movement: intransitive). I then used Amazon Mechanical Turk (AMT) to obtain transitivity judgments from non-signers, and correlated these judgments with features of the stimuli as well. Several features accurately predicted both actual and perceived transitivity, suggesting that: (a) without explicit training, non-signers recruit the same visual features for encoding and decoding transitivity in pantomime; and (b) the encoding of transitivity in classifier constructions and pantomime can be predicted using these same features. This suggests that these features have their roots in more domain general cognitive processes (e.g., vision, manual praxis), and suggests specific parameters for communicative strategies that collocutors try or principally do not try. The full dissertation can be accessed via the link above.

Bradley, C. (2013). Motion events and event segmentation in American Sign Language [Full ↗] [ Summary]

This project examined verbs of motion and location in American Sign Language (ASL) in light of semantic limits on the amount of spatial information spoken languages exhibit. Such verbs in ASL come in two sorts, lexical verbs (akin to words in spoken languages) and classifier constructions (aka depicting verbs), a set of highly iconic, polymorphemic signs. It was found that lexical verbs pattern like verbs of motion/location in spoken languages, while classifier constructions could provide much richer spatial descriptions. The project provides some discussion on whether path and location are gestural, linguistic or both in classifier constructions, but ultimately concludes that Language is not fully described without the inclusion of possibilities that are uniquely available to sign languages.

Bradley, C. (2009). A theoretical look at the Person Agreement Marker in German Sign Language. [ Summary]

This paper provided a syntactic treatment of the agreement marker, PAM, in German Sign Language. Specifically, despite Spell-Out traditionally being the locus of agreement phenomena (e.g., in Minimalism and Distributed Morphology), PAM interacts syntactically. I explained PAM's different linear positions by appealing to Remnant Movement, though the syntactic behavior of a post-syntactic agreement element was ultimately left unexplained.
